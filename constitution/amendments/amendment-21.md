# Amendment 21 — Repeal of Prohibition

*Proposed by Congress: February 20, 1933*
*Ratified: December 5, 1933*

## Text

**Section 1.** The eighteenth article of amendment to the Constitution of the United States is hereby repealed.

**Section 2.** The transportation or importation into any State, Territory, or possession of the United States for delivery or use therein of intoxicating liquors, in violation of the laws thereof, is hereby prohibited.

**Section 3.** This article shall be inoperative unless it shall have been ratified as an amendment to the Constitution by conventions in the several States, as provided in the Constitution, within seven years from the date of the submission hereof to the States by the Congress.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** By 1933, Prohibition had been an obvious failure for fourteen years. Organized crime had exploded. The federal government couldn't begin to enforce the Volstead Act. Corruption was endemic. And the Great Depression had created an urgent practical argument: legal alcohol would create jobs, generate tax revenue, and boost a devastated economy.

**Political climate:** Franklin Roosevelt had run in part on a repeal platform. The Democratic Party had championed repeal; the Republicans remained divided. Public opinion had shifted dramatically — surveys showed clear majority support for repeal.

**Notable procedural choice:** Congress chose to ratify via state conventions rather than state legislatures — only the second time in history this method was used (the first was the original Constitution itself). This was partly to avoid dry state legislatures blocking ratification, and partly a democratic gesture: convention delegates were elected specifically on the repeal question.

**What it changed:** Repealed the 18th Amendment. However, Section 2 gave states the right to ban alcohol within their borders — making the 21st Amendment also the constitutional basis for state and local alcohol regulation. "Dry counties" exist to this day in some states.

**Historical significance:** The 21st Amendment is the only constitutional amendment whose primary purpose was to repeal another. It stands as a monument to the principle that constitutional mistakes can be corrected — though it took 14 years and enormous social costs.
