# Amendment 27 — Congressional Pay (Delayed Effect)

*Originally proposed: September 25, 1789 (with the Bill of Rights)*
*Ratified: May 7, 1992 — 202 years, 7 months, 12 days after proposal*

## Text

No law, varying the compensation for the services of the Senators and Representatives, shall take effect, until an election of representatives shall have intervened.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was originally proposed:** James Madison included this amendment in his original set of 12 proposed amendments in 1789 (the other 10 became the Bill of Rights). The concern was straightforward: Congress shouldn't be able to vote themselves a pay raise that takes effect immediately. Citizens deserve a chance to vote on members who gave themselves a raise. Ten states ratified it in 1789-1791, then it languished for nearly two centuries.

**Why it was finally ratified in 1992:** In 1982, Gregory Watson, a 19-year-old University of Texas sophomore, discovered the unratified amendment while writing a term paper. His professor gave the paper a C. Watson began a one-man ratification campaign, writing to state legislators across the country. He pointed out that the original proposal had no ratification deadline — unlike most 20th-century amendments — which meant it was technically still pending.

**Political climate (1992):** Public anger about Congressional pay increases was at a peak. The House Banking Scandal (members overdrafting personal accounts at the House bank) and rising voter cynicism about Congress provided a perfect political environment for ratification. State legislatures rushed to ratify. Michigan provided the 38th and final vote on May 7, 1992.

**What it changed:** Any Congressional pay increase must wait until after the next House election to take effect. In practice, this means members who vote themselves a raise may not personally benefit from it.

**The longest story in constitutional history:** The 202-year gap between proposal and ratification is unprecedented. Constitutional scholars debated whether ratification after such an interval was valid; the archivist of the United States certified it, and Congress voted to accept the certification. It stands as the 27th Amendment.

**Gregory Watson's grade:** In 2017, after the story became widely known, Watson's UT professor — by then 84 years old — retroactively changed his grade to an A.
