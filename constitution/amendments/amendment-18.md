# Amendment 18 — Prohibition of Alcohol

*Proposed by Congress: December 18, 1917*
*Ratified: January 16, 1919*
*Repealed by: Amendment 21 (December 5, 1933)*

## Text

**Section 1.** After one year from the ratification of this article the manufacture, sale, or transportation of intoxicating liquors within, the importation thereof into, or the exportation thereof from the United States and all territory subject to the jurisdiction thereof for beverage purposes is hereby prohibited.

**Section 2.** The Congress and the several States shall have concurrent power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

**Section 3.** This article shall be inoperative unless it shall have been ratified as an amendment to the Constitution by the legislatures of the several States, as provided in the Constitution, within seven years from the date of the submission hereof to the States by the Congress.

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** The temperance movement had been building for nearly a century. The Women's Christian Temperance Union (founded 1874) and the Anti-Saloon League (founded 1893) had become enormously powerful political organizations. Saloons were genuinely associated with domestic violence, poverty, and political corruption (bosses used them to organize voters). World War I added a nationalist dimension: grain used for alcohol was grain not going to soldiers, and many breweries were German-owned.

**Political climate:** By 1916, 23 states had already enacted some form of prohibition. World War I created moral pressure for sacrifice and sobriety. The movement had support from both progressive reformers and conservative evangelical Protestants — an unusual coalition.

**What it changed:** Everything — and not what supporters expected. Organized crime exploded as bootleggers filled the vacuum. Al Capone and hundreds of other gangsters built empires. Speakeasies proliferated (30,000 in New York City alone). The federal government proved incapable of enforcement. Corruption spread through police forces and courts. Ironically, drinking among women increased, as speakeasies were more welcoming than saloons had been.

**The only amendment to be repealed:** The 18th is unique in US history — the only constitutional amendment that has been undone by another amendment (the 21st). Its failure is a canonical lesson in the limits of law to change behavior and the dangers of legislating morality.
