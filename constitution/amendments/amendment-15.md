# Amendment 15 — Right to Vote (Race)

*Proposed by Congress: February 26, 1869*
*Ratified: February 3, 1870*

## Text

**Section 1.** The right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of race, color, or previous condition of servitude.

**Section 2.** The Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** The third of the Reconstruction Amendments, the 15th directly addressed Black male suffrage. Although the 14th Amendment theoretically protected Black citizens' rights, states were finding workarounds to prevent Black men from voting. Literacy tests, poll taxes, grandfather clauses, and outright violence suppressed Black political participation. A constitutional guarantee was needed.

**Political climate:** The 1868 election demonstrated the political power of Black voters in the South — Ulysses Grant's victory depended on them. Republicans wanted to lock in this coalition constitutionally. There was significant debate about whether women should also be included; Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton broke with longtime allies over this, while Frederick Douglass argued that the moment required prioritizing Black male suffrage. The resulting amendment was limited to race, color, and previous servitude.

**What it changed:** Prohibited denial of voting rights based on race. For a brief period during Reconstruction, Black Americans voted and held office across the South. However, the end of Reconstruction in 1877 (with the withdrawal of federal troops) allowed Southern states to systematically disenfranchise Black voters for another 95 years through poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, white primaries, and terror.

**Significance today:** The Voting Rights Act of 1965 was necessary to make the 15th Amendment meaningful in practice — nearly a century after ratification. *Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) weakened the VRA's preclearance requirements, and voting rights remain actively contested.
