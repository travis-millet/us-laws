# Amendment 26 — Voting Age Lowered to 18

*Proposed by Congress: March 23, 1971*
*Ratified: July 1, 1971 (fastest ratification in US history — 100 days)*

## Text

**Section 1.** The right of citizens of the United States, who are eighteen years of age or older, to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of age.

**Section 2.** The Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** The Vietnam War created the central political pressure: young men were being drafted at 18 to fight and die in Vietnam but couldn't vote for the politicians who sent them there. The slogan "old enough to fight, old enough to vote" became a powerful rallying cry. The voting age had been 21 in most states.

**Political background:** Congress had tried a simpler route first. The Voting Rights Act of 1970 included a provision lowering the voting age to 18 for all elections. The Supreme Court, in *Oregon v. Mitchell* (1970), upheld this for federal elections but struck it down for state elections — a messy split ruling that required some states to maintain two sets of voter rolls. A constitutional amendment was the only clean solution.

**Political climate:** The 26th Amendment moved through Congress and ratification with remarkable speed — 100 days from proposal to ratification, faster than any other amendment. Both parties saw electoral incentive: Democrats hoped young voters would be anti-war and left-leaning; Republicans supported it as a matter of principle and hoped to attract young voters.

**What it changed:** Added approximately 11 million new voters in 1972. Contrary to both parties' expectations, young voters have historically participated at lower rates than older voters and their partisan preferences have varied by election cycle.

**Significance today:** The 26th Amendment remains the most recently ratified substantive amendment (the 27th, ratified in 1992, is largely procedural). Youth voter turnout has become a major factor in recent elections.
