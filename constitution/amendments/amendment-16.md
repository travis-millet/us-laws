# Amendment 16 — Federal Income Tax

*Proposed by Congress: July 12, 1909*
*Ratified: February 3, 1913*

## Text

The Congress shall have power to lay and collect taxes on incomes, from whatever source derived, without apportionment among the several States, and without regard to any census or enumeration.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** Congress had enacted an income tax to fund the Civil War, but *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust Co.* (1895) struck down a peacetime income tax as an unconstitutional "direct tax" that had to be apportioned among states by population — making it practically unworkable. As industrialization concentrated wealth among a small number of robber barons, pressure grew for a progressive income tax to replace tariffs (which fell disproportionately on working-class consumers).

**Political climate:** The Progressive Era was in full swing. President Taft, typically conservative, supported the amendment partly as a political maneuver to forestall a more radical tariff reform bill. Progressives like Robert La Follette championed it as a way to tax concentrated wealth. It required four years of state ratification before reaching the three-fourths threshold.

**What it changed:** Enabled the modern federal government. Without the income tax, the federal government could not fund World War I, the New Deal, World War II, or any of the major programs of the 20th century. The IRS was created to administer it.

**Significance today:** The federal income tax now generates roughly half of all federal revenue. The 16th Amendment is the constitutional basis for all federal income taxation.
