# Amendment 24 — Abolition of Poll Taxes

*Proposed by Congress: August 27, 1962*
*Ratified: January 23, 1964*

## Text

**Section 1.** The right of citizens of the United States to vote in any primary or other election for President or Vice President, for electors for President or Vice President, or for Senator or Representative in Congress, shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or any State by reason of failure to pay any poll tax or other tax.

**Section 2.** The Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** Poll taxes — fees required to vote — had been used since Reconstruction as a tool to disenfranchise Black Americans and poor white voters in Southern states. Five states still had poll taxes in 1962: Virginia, Alabama, Mississippi, Arkansas, and Texas. These taxes were modest in dollar amount but represented real barriers for sharecroppers and low-income workers, and were often administered discriminatorily.

**Political climate:** The civil rights movement was at peak intensity. The Freedom Rides (1961), the Birmingham Campaign (1963), and March on Washington (1963) were creating enormous pressure for federal action. President Kennedy had proposed the amendment as a step toward voting rights reform. It was a relatively uncontroversial voting rights measure — the more contentious battles were being fought over segregation and the broader Voting Rights Act.

**What it changed:** Eliminated poll taxes for federal elections. However, the amendment only covered federal elections — not state and local elections. *Harper v. Virginia Board of Elections* (1966) extended the prohibition to state elections via the Equal Protection Clause.

**Connection to broader civil rights legislation:** The 24th Amendment was ratified in January 1964 — the same year as the Civil Rights Act (July 1964). The Voting Rights Act of 1965 followed, providing enforcement mechanisms that the 15th Amendment had lacked for 95 years.
