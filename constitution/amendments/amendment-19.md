# Amendment 19 — Women's Suffrage

*Proposed by Congress: June 4, 1919*
*Ratified: August 18, 1920*

## Text

The right of citizens of the United States to vote shall not be denied or abridged by the United States or by any State on account of sex.

Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** The women's suffrage movement had been fighting for 72 years since the Seneca Falls Convention (1848), where Elizabeth Cady Stanton and others drafted the Declaration of Sentiments demanding voting rights. Progress had been slow: Wyoming Territory granted women's suffrage in 1869, and by 1919, 15 states had full women's suffrage. But federal action was needed to make it universal.

**Political climate:** World War I was a turning point. Women's contributions to the war effort — working in factories, volunteering as nurses, buying war bonds — made it politically untenable to deny them the vote. President Woodrow Wilson, originally opposed, reversed his position in 1918. The suffragists' tactics had escalated: pickets at the White House, hunger strikes, and the brutal "Night of Terror" (November 15, 1917), when suffragists imprisoned at Occoquan Workhouse were beaten and force-fed, generated enormous public sympathy.

**Key moment:** The Tennessee state legislature provided the final ratification vote on August 18, 1920. The deciding vote came from Harry Burn, a 24-year-old representative who had received a note from his mother that morning urging him to "be a good boy" and vote for ratification. He changed his vote, giving women the right to vote.

**What it changed:** Roughly doubled the potential electorate overnight. Women voted in the 1920 election just two months later. However, the 19th Amendment's protections applied primarily to white women — Black women in the South faced the same disenfranchisement tactics (poll taxes, literacy tests, violence) used against Black men, and wouldn't achieve full voting rights until the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

**Significance today:** The 19th Amendment is the constitutional guarantee of women's right to vote. The intersectional critique — that it primarily benefited white women — has become an important part of how historians interpret the suffrage movement.
