# Amendment 11 — Judicial Limits (Suits Against States)

*Proposed by Congress: March 4, 1794*
*Ratified: February 7, 1795*

## Text

The Judicial power of the United States shall not be construed to extend to any suit in law or equity, commenced or prosecuted against one of the United States by Citizens of another State, or by Citizens or Subjects of any Foreign State.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** In 1793, the Supreme Court ruled in *Chisholm v. Georgia* that a South Carolina citizen could sue the state of Georgia in federal court to recover debts from the Revolutionary War era. Georgia refused to appear, and the ruling outraged states across the country. State sovereignty — the idea that a state could not be dragged into federal court by a private citizen — was seen as fundamental. The backlash was immediate and fierce.

**Political climate:** The ruling landed in the middle of fierce Federalist vs. Anti-Federalist debates about the proper balance of federal and state power. States feared the decision would expose them to floods of debt-collection lawsuits from war creditors. Congress moved with unusual speed: this amendment was proposed and ratified in under a year.

**What it changed:** Effectively overruled *Chisholm v. Georgia*. Established the doctrine of "sovereign immunity" — the principle that a state cannot be sued in federal court by citizens of another state or foreign nationals without the state's consent. Later extended by Supreme Court interpretation to also cover suits by a state's own citizens (see *Hans v. Louisiana*, 1890).

**Significance today:** The 11th Amendment is the foundation of state sovereign immunity doctrine and frequently litigated in cases involving civil rights claims against state governments.
