# Amendment 13 — Abolition of Slavery

*Proposed by Congress: January 31, 1865*
*Ratified: December 6, 1865*

## Text

**Section 1.** Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude, except as a punishment for crime whereof the party shall have been duly convicted, shall exist within the United States, or any place subject to their jurisdiction.

**Section 2.** Congress shall have power to enforce this article by appropriate legislation.

---

## Historical Context

**Why it was passed:** The Emancipation Proclamation of January 1, 1863 had freed enslaved people in Confederate states as a war measure — but it was an executive order, not a law, and it didn't apply to border states still in the Union (Kentucky, Missouri, Maryland, Delaware). There was serious legal doubt about whether it would survive the war or survive Lincoln's presidency. To permanently end slavery everywhere in the United States required a constitutional amendment.

**Political climate:** The Civil War had fundamentally changed the political calculus. The abolitionist movement, which had been a radical minority position for decades, had moved toward the political mainstream through the war's carnage and Lincoln's evolution on the issue. The Republican Party controlled Congress. Lincoln actively lobbied for the amendment, knowing the war's end might reduce momentum. The film *Lincoln* (2012) dramatizes the final weeks of lobbying needed to get the two-thirds House majority.

**Key dates:**
- January 31, 1865: Passed House 119-56 (after narrowly failing in 1864)
- April 9, 1865: Confederate General Lee surrenders at Appomattox
- April 14, 1865: Lincoln assassinated
- December 6, 1865: Ratification completed (Georgia's approval provides 27th of needed states)

**What it changed:** Abolished slavery and involuntary servitude throughout the United States. This ended an institution that had existed in American territory since 1619 — 246 years. The exception clause ("except as a punishment for crime") has been criticized for enabling prison labor and is the subject of ongoing legal and political debate.

**Significance today:** The 13th Amendment is one of the three Reconstruction Amendments (13th, 14th, 15th) that fundamentally rewrote the constitutional order after the Civil War. The "punishment for crime" exception has become increasingly debated in the context of mass incarceration and prison labor.
